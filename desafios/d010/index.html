Android Mascot

You probably know that the Android operating system, maintained by Google, is one of the most used for mobile devices in the world. But maybe you don't know that your friendly mascot has a name and a very curious story? Well, keep reading this article to learn a lot about this little robot.

The first version

The first attempt to create a mascot came up in 2007 from a developer called Dan Morrill. He says that he used Inkscape (free software for vectorizing images) and created his own version of a robot. The goal was just to personify the system just for his team, there was no request from the company to create a mascot.

This first bizarre version was even named in honor of its creator: the Dandroids.

A new mascot appears

The idea of having a mascot matured and the mission was handed over to a professional in the area. The Russian illustrator Irina Blok, also Google`s employee, got the 'misison' to represent the little robot in a more pleasant way.

Irina's main idea was to represent everything graphically with few strokes and in a flatter way. The drawing should also generate quick identification with the viewer. Then came the Bugdroid, Android's new mascot.

The main inspiration for the features of the new Bugdroid came up from those little dolls that illustrate toilets doors to indicate the gender of each door. Legend has it that the artist was creating on her desk at Google's office and looked towards the bathrooms and the identification was immediate: simple, clean, objective.

Do you want to learn more?

Another curious thing about Android is that each version has always been named after a sweet, in alphabetical order from version 1.5 to 9.0.

  • 1.5 - Cupcake
  • 1.6 - Donut
  • 3.0 - Eclair
  • 2.2 - Froyo
  • 2.3 - Gingerbread
  • 3.0 - Honeycomb
  • 4.0 - Ice Cream Sandwich
  • 4.1 - Jelly Bean
  • 4.4 - KitKat
  • 5.0 - Lolipop
  • 6.0 - Marshmallow
  • 7.0 - Nougat
  • 8.0 - Oreo
  • 9.0 - Pie

Unfortunately, Android Q did not exist, as Google decided to put an end to this fun practice and started using numbers, which gave rise to Android 10.

Dive into the Android History website to know the sequence of the "sweetened" versions and what each one brought to the Android system.
